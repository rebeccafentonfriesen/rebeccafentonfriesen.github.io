Brain Machine Interfaces

Sep 1, 2013

After finishing college, I worked for several years as a technician in a neural engineering lab. At that time, paralyzed patients could carefully move a screen cursor or robot arm in a desired direction using direct neural control from cortical arrays recording from approximately 100 motor neurons. However, movements were often painsakingly slow, shaky, and limited in their ability to generalize to other tasks. Users had to stare intently at the end effector they were trying to move, as they had zero sense of grasp, contact, or location in space. Providing sensation via cortical or even nerve ending stimulation was in its infancy; researchers were trying to ascertain whether stimulation could be felt at all, and effects were often described as “buzzy” or “unpleasant”. Working in this field highlighted for me not only the critical importance of sensory feedback in performing skilled movements, but also the challenging problem of making such feedback feel natural and good.
